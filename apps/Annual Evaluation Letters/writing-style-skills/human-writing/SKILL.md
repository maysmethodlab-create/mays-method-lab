# Human Writing Skill

## When to Use

Apply this skill whenever you are writing or revising prose intended for human readers — academic papers, cover letters, memos, reviews, proposals, or any document where AI-sounding text would undermine credibility. Load this skill BEFORE drafting. Run the checklist AFTER each draft.

## The Core Principle

AI writing lacks a situated speaker. It emerges from nowhere, addressed to no one, with no stake in its claims. Human writing reveals a person with specific knowledge, specific ignorance, and specific reasons for writing. Every rule below serves this principle.

---

## CATEGORY A: BANNED WORDS (Replace with plain alternatives)

### A1. Banned adjectives
Never use: robust (non-statistical), comprehensive, nuanced, multifaceted, intricate, innovative, cutting-edge, seamless, pivotal, crucial, vital, vibrant, compelling, profound, notable, commendable, meticulous, versatile, holistic

**Instead:** Use the plainest accurate word. "Important" not "pivotal." "Thorough" not "comprehensive." "Complicated" not "multifaceted." If no adjective is needed, cut it entirely.

### A2. Banned verbs
Never use: delve, harness, leverage, underscore, foster, enhance, streamline, optimize, embark, navigate, unpack, unravel, showcase, garner, spearhead, bolster, catalyze, revolutionize, transcend

**Instead:** "Use" not "leverage." "Show" not "showcase." "Start" not "embark." "Strengthen" not "bolster." Prefer one-syllable verbs when they work.

### A3. Banned nouns
Never use: landscape, realm, ecosystem (non-biological), tapestry, trajectory (non-physical), paradigm, synergy, nexus, interplay, cornerstone, bedrock, underpinning, testament, beacon, hallmark, game-changer, paradigm shift

**Instead:** Say what you actually mean. "The AI industry" not "the AI landscape." "Connection" not "nexus." "Foundation" not "bedrock."

### A4. Banned adverbs
Never use as filler: fundamentally, significantly (without a test), remarkably, notably, importantly, crucially, essentially, ultimately, inherently

**Instead:** If the adverb is doing no work, delete it. If it is doing work, replace it with evidence. "Revenue fell 30%" not "Revenue fell significantly."

## CATEGORY B: BANNED PHRASES AND OPENERS

### B1. Throat-clearing openers (never start a sentence or paragraph with these)
- "In today's [anything]..."
- "In an era of..."
- "In the ever-evolving..."
- "As [technology/field] continues to evolve..."
- "It is important to note that..."
- "It is worth noting that..."
- "It bears mentioning that..."
- "It should be noted that..."
- "Let us now turn to..."
- "In this section, we will discuss..."

**Instead:** Start with the actual point. Delete the opener and begin with the substance.

### B2. Banned transitions (never use as sentence openers)
- "Moreover,"
- "Furthermore,"
- "Additionally,"
- "Indeed,"
- "Notably,"
- "Importantly,"
- "Consequently," (at sentence start)
- "Taken together,"
- "In summary,"
- "In conclusion," (as a section opener)

**Instead:** Let the logical connection be implicit, or use a specific connective that names the relationship: "This result contradicts..." / "A second factor is..." / "The same pattern appears in..."

### B3. Banned constructions
- "In other words,..." — if you need to restate, rewrite the original sentence to be clearer
- "the fact that" — rephrase: "the fact that costs rose" becomes "rising costs" or "because costs rose"
- "In order to" — just use "to"
- "Due to the fact that" — use "because"
- "At this point in time" — use "now"
- "It is widely recognized that..." — say who recognizes it and cite them
- "Scholars have long debated..." — name the scholars

## CATEGORY C: STRUCTURAL RULES

### C1. No symmetric load-balancing
Paragraphs and sections should vary in length based on the complexity of the idea, not be artificially equalized. A paragraph can be two sentences if the point is simple. A paragraph can be twelve sentences if the argument is layered.

### C2. No rule-of-three default
Do not default to three items in every list, three adjectives in every description, three examples in every illustration. List however many items actually exist — two, four, seven. The number should reflect reality, not rhetorical convention.

### C3. No formulaic paragraph structure
Do not write every paragraph as: topic sentence, three supporting points, summary sentence. Vary paragraph openings — start with an example, a question, a concession, a piece of evidence. Some paragraphs should not have a topic sentence at all.

### C4. Maximum one em dash per 1,000 words
Em dashes are the most recognizable AI punctuation pattern. Use commas, parentheses, semicolons, colons, or separate sentences instead. Reserve the rare em dash for genuine emphasis.

### C5. No triple parallel structures
Do not use three phrases with identical grammatical structure in sequence. "AI changes how firms compete, how firms organize, and how firms learn" — restructure so the syntax varies.

### C6. No "While X, Y" openers more than once per page
"While firms invest heavily, returns remain elusive" is fine once. Twice on the same page creates a noticeable pattern.

### C7. No vague "This [abstract noun]" at sentence start
"This finding..." / "This approach..." / "This dynamic..." — specify what "this" refers to. "The decline in override rates suggests..." not "This suggests..."

## CATEGORY D: SENTENCE-LEVEL RULES

### D1. No present-participial tacking
Do not end sentences with "-ing" phrases that summarize significance: "...highlighting the importance of governance," "...underscoring the need for alignment," "...symbolizing the broader shift toward automation." These are AI tells at 2-5x the rate of human writing. End the sentence before the participial phrase. If the point is worth making, give it its own sentence.

### D2. Prefer verbs over nominalizations
"The system improved outcomes" not "The implementation of the system led to an improvement in outcomes." If you can replace a noun-phrase with a verb, do it.

### D3. No copula avoidance
Use "is" and "are" freely. Do not replace them with "serves as," "stands as," "represents," "constitutes," "functions as," or "operates as" unless the sentence genuinely requires the distinction. "Berlin is the capital" not "Berlin serves as the vibrant capital."

### D4. Limit the "not just X, but Y" construction
Use at most once per section. It becomes a rhythmic tic when repeated.

### D5. No synonym cycling (elegant variation)
Repeat the same word if it is the right word. "The study... the study... the study" is better than "the study... the research... the investigation... the inquiry." Clarity beats variety.

### D6. No excessive hedging
Match confidence to evidence. If the data are strong, write with conviction. "Costs rose 40%" not "It could potentially be argued that costs may have experienced an increase." Calibrate uncertainty honestly — hedge where genuinely uncertain, assert where the evidence supports assertion.

### D7. No anthropomorphized research
Research does not "reveal," "suggest," "underscore," "highlight," or "shed light on" anything. Researchers do. Data show patterns. Studies find results. The study did not "uncover a surprising finding" — the authors found something unexpected.

## CATEGORY E: RHETORICAL AND TONAL RULES

### E1. Take positions
Do not present false balance. If the evidence strongly supports one interpretation, say so. "The evidence favors X" is better than "While some argue X, others contend Y" when Y has weak support. Academic writing requires judgment, not neutrality.

### E2. Show epistemic variation
Be certain about some things and uncertain about others. Flat, uniform confidence across all claims signals a non-human source. A human expert writes "We are confident that X [strong evidence], though Y remains unclear [limited data]."

### E3. No signposting announcements
Do not write "In this section, we will discuss..." Just begin discussing. The reader will follow. Rare exceptions: a roadmap paragraph at the end of an introduction.

### E4. Cite specifically
"Research suggests" is not a citation. Name the authors and year. "Vague attribution to unnamed consensus" is an AI tell. If you cannot name a specific source, you may not have a basis for the claim.

### E5. No generic positive conclusions
Do not end with vague optimism: "As the field continues to evolve, these innovations promise to transform..." End with what was found, what remains unknown, and what should be done next — specifically.

### E6. Leave appropriate loose ends
Not everything resolves neatly. Human writers flag unresolved tensions, admit limitations, and say "I am not sure what to make of this" when warranted. AI wraps everything up. Resist the urge to tie a bow on every paragraph.

---

## CATEGORY F: FORMATTING RULES

### F1. No boldfaced inline headers in running prose
The pattern of **Bold term:** followed by a description is the single most recognizable AI formatting tell. In academic prose, integrate terms into sentences.

### F2. Use sentence case in headings
"The return on AI framework" not "The Return On AI Framework" (unless the publication style guide requires title case).

### F3. No overuse of boldface
Reserve bold for truly critical terms introduced for the first time. Do not mechanically bold every technical term or acronym.

## SELF-REVIEW CHECKLIST

After drafting, run this checklist. Fix every violation before presenting the text.

1. Search for every word in the banned lists (A1-A4). Replace or delete.
2. Check every sentence opener against the banned openers (B1-B2). Rewrite.
3. Search for banned constructions (B3). Rephrase.
4. Count em dashes. Reduce to at most 1 per 1,000 words (C4).
5. Check for triple parallel structures (C5). Vary the syntax.
6. Check for participial tacking at sentence ends (D1). Cut or restructure.
7. Check for synonym cycling (D5). Let words repeat.
8. Check for anthropomorphized research (D7). Fix the subject.
9. Check for vague "This [noun]" openers (C7). Specify the referent.
10. Read the opening and closing sentences of each paragraph. Do they sound like a human expert speaking to a colleague, or like a machine producing text? Rewrite until they sound human.
