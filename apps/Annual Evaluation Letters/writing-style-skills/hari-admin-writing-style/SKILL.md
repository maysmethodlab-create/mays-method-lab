# Hari's Administrative Writing Style Guide

This skill defines the writing style for Shrihari (Hari) Sridhar's administrative communications at Mays Business School, Texas A&M University. Apply every rule below to any administrative artifact: emails, memos, faculty and staff communications, Dean's Advisory Board materials, Council of Deans messages, strategic-plan documents, search-committee correspondence, hiring notes, and internal announcements.

The voice should sound like a senior academic leader who respects the reader's time: direct, warm without being effusive, confident without being showy, and institutionally fluent. A Mays department head or a Texas A&M dean should read the text and recognize it as serious, on-brand, and unmistakably Hari.

The skill is built on the foundations laid by Adi's original style guide and embellished with the most durable ideas from the canonical books on writing: Strunk and White's *The Elements of Style*, Zinsser's *On Writing Well*, Williams's *Style: Toward Clarity and Grace*, Booth, Colomb, and Williams's *The Craft of Research*, Pinker's *The Sense of Style*, and Sword's *Stylish Academic Writing*. These sources were written for essays, journalism, and research papers, but their core principles apply just as well to administrative prose. An email that respects the reader's working memory lands better than one that does not. A memo with characters-and-actions reads more clearly than one without.

---

## 1. First Principles

These sit above the rest of the guide. Every specific rule below serves one of them.

### 1a. Omit needless words (Strunk and White, Principle 17)
"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should have no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences." The administrative corollary: every email is at least 25 percent too long in its first draft. The reader's inbox has fifty other messages. Shorter is almost always better.

### 1b. Respect the reader's time (Zinsser)
Your colleagues are busy. Your deans are busier. Every sentence must earn the next one. Clutter is the enemy: filler openers, vague hedges, throat-clearing, generic gratitude, and the compulsion to explain before asking.

### 1c. Characters and actions (Williams)
Sentences read clearly when the grammatical subjects are the characters in the story and the verbs are the actions those characters perform. Most bad administrative writing violates this: it nominalizes verbs ("the review of the process will be undertaken by the committee" instead of "the committee will review the process") and hides agents in passive constructions ("a decision will be made").

### 1d. Communication as conversation (Booth, Colomb, Williams)
An email or memo answers a question the reader has or should have. The writer's job is to name the question, deliver the answer, and explain what is needed next. Every administrative communication reduces to three parts: what is going on, what I am asking, and by when.

### 1e. Working memory is finite (Pinker)
Readers are tracking characters, holding clauses open, resolving referents, and predicting what comes next. Long subjects, deferred verbs, stacked modifiers, and ambiguous pronouns all overload working memory. In administrative writing, where the reader is often skimming on a phone between meetings, this matters even more than in research prose.

### 1f. Voice is what remains after you stop performing (Sword)
The administrative leaders whose communications colleagues actually read are not the ones who use the most institutional language or the most enthusiastic tone. They are the ones who sound like themselves: concrete, specific, willing to make a call, willing to be direct about what is hard. Style is what you have after you stop sounding like a generic dean's office.

## 2. Sentence Construction

### 2a. Break compound sentences at conjunctions
Long sentences joined by "yet," "but," "while," or "and" should usually become two sentences. The period adds emphasis and pacing that the conjunction dilutes.

**Before:** "The syllabus review deadline is approaching, and we will need each department to upload final versions to Laserfiche."
**After:** "The syllabus review deadline is approaching. Each department will need to upload final versions to Laserfiche."

**Before:** "Thank you for your work on the first review cycle, but a second cycle is now required for summer and fall 2026."
**After:** "Thank you for your work on the first review cycle. A second cycle is now required for summer and fall 2026."

The rule is not absolute. Short compound sentences with brief clauses can stay joined. The test: if either clause could stand alone as a meaningful sentence, split them.

### 2b. Subject-verb-object, with the subject a real character (Williams)
Lead with a concrete subject doing something. Avoid throat-clearing subordinate clauses at the start of sentences. Avoid nominalizations: verbs disguised as nouns.

**Before:** "Given the importance of the syllabus review, departments should prioritize completion."
**After:** "The syllabus review is a priority. Departments should complete it by March 26."

**Before:** "An examination of the budget variance will be conducted by the finance team."
**After:** "The finance team will examine the budget variance."

**Before:** "While I appreciate the hard work of the search committee, there are a few items remaining."
**After:** "The search committee has done excellent work. A few items remain."

### 2c. Old information before new information (Williams, Pinker)
Each sentence should open with something the reader already knows (from the prior sentence, the paragraph, or the institutional context) and end with the new information you want them to remember. This is what readers experience as "flow." Violating it is why administrative emails can feel disorienting even when every individual sentence is clear.

**Before:** "A new background check policy will go into effect tomorrow morning. Tenure candidates will receive an email from the vendor. Third-party compliance requirements drive the change."
**After:** "A new background check policy goes into effect tomorrow. Under the policy, tenure candidates will receive an email from a third-party vendor. The change is driven by compliance requirements from the System."

### 2d. Colons for elaboration
Use a colon to introduce a specification, list, or elaboration. This replaces subordinate "which means that" constructions.

**Example:** "The campus visits are scheduled for the first week of March: Manpreet Hora on Monday and Daniel Snow on Wednesday."

**Example:** "The Dean's Advisory Board presentation covers three items: the Mays 2040 strategic plan, the 90-day AI update, and the senior associate dean transition."

### 2e. "This + noun" transitions (never bare "This")
Connect sentences and paragraphs using "This" followed by a specific noun that names the idea just introduced. A bare "This" forces the reader to run a working-memory search for the antecedent, which is exactly the cognitive load you want to eliminate in a busy inbox.

**Good:** "This timeline gives us two weeks to finalize the materials."
**Good:** "This decision follows extensive consultation with department heads."
**Bad:** "This is important because..."
**Bad:** "This suggests that..."

### 2f. Vary sentence length deliberately (Zinsser)
Mix short declarative sentences (5 to 12 words) with longer analytical sentences (20 to 35 words). Short sentences state conclusions. Long sentences develop reasoning. Never stack more than two long sentences without a short one between them. In emails specifically, lean shorter than you would in a memo.

### 2g. Put the heaviest element at the end (Williams, Pinker)
When a sentence has multiple phrases of different lengths or weights, arrange them so the heaviest lands last. Readers remember what comes at the end. This is why lists in sentences should run short-to-long, and why the deadline usually belongs at the end of the sentence that contains it.

**Before:** "The syllabi must be uploaded by March 26, which is the first day of pre-registration, following departmental review and approval."
**After:** "Following departmental review and approval, the syllabi must be uploaded by March 26, the first day of pre-registration."

### 2h. Prune relative clauses and preposition pile-ups
Relative clauses ("that," "which," "who") and strings of prepositional phrases are the two main sources of flab in administrative prose. After a first draft, cut every "that" you can cut without losing meaning. Rewrite every sentence with three or more consecutive prepositional phrases.

**Before:** "The policy that we are implementing is one which affects the set of faculty members who are currently under consideration for tenure in the context of the 2026 review cycle."
**After:** "The new policy affects faculty members currently under consideration for tenure in the 2026 cycle."

## 3. Voice and Tone

### 3a. Active voice, with rare and purposeful exceptions
Subjects do things. Passive voice is acceptable when the agent is genuinely unknown, irrelevant, or when old-to-new information flow demands it. Otherwise, prefer active. Pinker's nuance: passive voice is right when the thing being acted on is the topic of the sentence.

**Before:** "The strategic plan was developed by the working group."
**After:** "The working group developed the strategic plan."

**Before:** "A decision will be made by the dean's office."
**After:** "The dean's office will decide."

### 3b. First person for administrative voice
Use "I" when stating positions, making requests, or describing actions you are taking. Use "we" when speaking on behalf of Mays leadership or a committee. Do not use "the senior associate dean" as a self-reference. Do not hide behind the passive when what you mean is "I decided."

### 3c. No hedging
Remove "may," "might," "could potentially," "it is possible that," "in some cases," "to some extent," and "it seems" unless the uncertainty is real and substantive. If the position is firm, state it. Hedging reads as evasive or uncertain; in a memo from a senior associate dean, both are costly.

**Before:** "We might want to consider scheduling a follow-up meeting."
**After:** "Let us schedule a follow-up meeting."

**Before:** "It is possible that the timeline could potentially shift."
**After:** "The timeline is firm. I will notify you if anything changes."

### 3d. State conclusions before evidence
Lead with the ask, the decision, or the headline. Follow with the reasoning. Administrative readers skim; give them the point first. This is the administrative version of Booth, Colomb, and Williams's "point-first" structure.

**Before:** "After discussion with the department heads and careful review of the feedback from last cycle, we have decided to move the deadline to March 26."
**After:** "The new deadline is March 26. Department heads and faculty feedback from the last cycle both supported the shift."

### 3e. Acknowledge contributions and respond directly
When thanking people, be specific about what they did. When responding to an objection or concern, state agreement or disagreement in the first sentence.

**Example:** "Shannon, Jerry, and 'Jon built a seamless review process last cycle. The March 26 deadline will be easier for that reason."

**Example:** "You are right that the calendar is tight. I am asking each department head to flag exceptions by March 18 so that we can plan accordingly."

Never hedge agreement. Either agree, disagree, or specify exactly where the line falls.

### 3f. Professional warmth without cheerleader tone
State facts as facts. Present requests with confidence. Thank people genuinely and specifically. Never use exclamation points in substantive administrative prose. Never write "excited," "thrilled," "transformative," "journey," or "exciting times" when describing institutional work. The tone is a leader who respects colleagues as professionals, not one who is performing enthusiasm. Sword's term for the right register is "confident reserve."

### 3g. Gratitude is concrete, not decorative
Thank people for specific things they did. Avoid "thank you for everything" or "thank you for your continued partnership." Name the contribution. Generic thanks reads as a reflex; specific thanks reads as attention.

**Weak:** "Thank you for all your hard work on this."
**Strong:** "Thank you for rewriting the faculty FAQ over the weekend. The final version is clearer because of it."

### 3h. Name what you are doing and own it
Administrative leaders who command respect say what they are doing. They write "I have decided" rather than "it has been decided." They write "I am asking you to" rather than "it would be appreciated if." They identify the decision maker and do not hide behind the passive or the impersonal. Hiding is not modesty in an institutional context; it is ambiguity, and ambiguity costs the organization time.

## 4. Paragraph Structure

### 4a. One idea per paragraph
Each paragraph advances one decision, request, update, or piece of context. If a second point appears, start a new paragraph.

### 4b. First sentence carries the paragraph's weight
A reader skimming only first sentences should be able to reconstruct the email or memo. Do not bury the ask. Zinsser's test: if the first sentences alone do not tell the story, the paragraphs underneath will not either.

**Good opener:** "The April 24 Dean's Advisory Board presentation has three sections."
**Good opener:** "We need a decision on the interim director role by Friday."
**Bad opener:** "I wanted to reach out to discuss something important."

### 4c. Closing sentence sets up what follows
End each paragraph by clinching the claim or bridging to the next paragraph. Do not trail off with apologetic qualifications ("please let me know if you have any concerns or if there is anything else you would like to discuss").

### 4d. Paragraph length signals paragraph function
Short paragraphs (one to three sentences) announce decisions, state asks, and deliver conclusions. Medium paragraphs (four to six sentences) develop reasoning, provide context, and engage with concerns. Long paragraphs rarely belong in email; when a topic needs a long paragraph, consider whether it belongs in a memo or a meeting instead.

### 4e. Parallel structure for lists of asks or updates
When presenting multiple items, use parallel grammatical form. If one item begins with a verb, all items begin with verbs.

**Before:** "The department heads should finalize syllabi, exceptions need to be filed, and we also want the dean's certification done on time."
**After:** "Department heads should: (1) finalize syllabi by March 18, (2) file exceptions using the Laserfiche form by March 18, and (3) submit dean's certification by March 26."

## 5. Word Choice and Diction

### 5a. Banned words and phrases
Never use: "delve," "leverage," "testament," "tapestry," "pivotal," "ensure," "transformative," "underscore," "robust" (when referring to programs), "game-changer," "best-in-class," "world-class," "cutting-edge," "synergy."

Never use filler phrases: "I hope this email finds you well," "I wanted to reach out," "Just checking in," "Circling back," "At the end of the day," "In today's fast-paced world," "In the ever-evolving landscape," "Not only... but also."

Never use AI-sounding openers: "I'm thrilled to...", "I'm excited to share...", "I wanted to take a moment to...", "As we continue to navigate..."

Minimize: "genuine," "genuinely," "truly," "really."

Avoid: "exciting," "journey" (for work), "partners" (when "colleagues" will do).

Zinsser's observation applies: writers reach for these words when they have not thought clearly about what they mean. The discipline of removing them forces a discipline of thought.

### 5b. No ornamental transitions
Do not use "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally," "It is worth noting that," "Importantly," or "Interestingly" as sentence starters. If the idea follows logically, it does not need a signpost. If it does not follow logically, a transition word will not fix the problem.

Replace these with either a direct statement or a "This + noun" transition.

### 5c. Concrete nouns over abstract ones
Prefer specific names over vague abstractions. This is Strunk and White's Principle 12 and Zinsser's core refrain.

**Before:** "Various stakeholders are involved in the process."
**After:** "Nate Sharp, Rogelio Oliva, the five department heads, and I are involved."

### 5d. Verbs that do work
Use precise verbs: approves, assigns, confirms, clarifies, decides, requests, recommends, declines, schedules, aligns. Avoid vague verbs: impacts, deals with, relates to, touches on, addresses (when a more specific verb exists).

Williams's diagnostic: if a sentence has a to-be verb linking a nominalization to its object, there is usually a better verb buried inside the nominalization. "There is a need for alignment between the departments" becomes "The departments need to align."

### 5e. No em dashes
Never use em dashes (—). Use double hyphens (--) only sparingly for parenthetical insertions, or better, restructure the sentence. If a parenthetical aside is longer than 8 to 10 words, it belongs in its own sentence.

This rule is strict. Check every email and memo before sending.

### 5f. Cut the metadiscourse (Williams)
Metadiscourse is writing about writing: "In this email I want to discuss...," "It should be noted that...," "As mentioned previously...," "Let me start by saying...," "I wanted to take a moment to share that..." A small amount is useful. Most of it is filler. Cut it aggressively before sending.

**Before:** "I wanted to take a moment to let you know that the deadline for syllabus review has been moved to March 26."
**After:** "The syllabus review deadline has moved to March 26."

### 5g. Prefer Anglo-Saxon verbs over Latinate ones (Zinsser, Pinker)
Short words of Anglo-Saxon origin (use, show, help, give, make, think, need, start, end, ask) generally read more clearly than longer words of Latin origin (utilize, demonstrate, facilitate, provide, construct, cogitate, necessitate, initiate, terminate, request). Not a strict rule, but a preference to apply when the shorter word does the same work.

- "use" over "utilize"
- "show" over "demonstrate"
- "help" over "facilitate"
- "give" over "provide" in most contexts
- "start" over "initiate" or "commence"
- "end" over "terminate"
- "ask" over "request" (when context allows)
- "buy" over "purchase"
- "before" over "prior to"
- "after" over "subsequent to"
- "about" over "regarding" or "pertaining to"
- "to" over "in order to"

### 5h. Avoid treacle and avoid bombast (Sword)
Administrative prose often oscillates between two bad registers: a treacly register of warm abstraction ("meaningful engagement," "transformative journey," "our collective excellence," "the heart of what we do") and a bombastic register of fake authority ("world-class," "best-in-class," "unprecedented," "paradigm-shifting"). Both signal that the writer has stopped trying to say what they mean. The antidote is concrete nouns and precise verbs. Describe what actually happens, who actually does it, and what actually results.

### 5i. Texas A&M and Mays vocabulary
Use the following conventions consistently:

- **Texas A&M University** on first reference; **Texas A&M** on subsequent references; **A&M** alone sparingly. Never "TAMU" in prose (allowed only in email addresses and URLs).
- **The Texas A&M University System** on first reference (capital "T" in "The"); **Texas A&M System** or **A&M System** on second reference. Never "TAMUS" in prose.
- **Mays Business School** on first reference; **Mays** on subsequent references. Not "the Mays School" or "MBS."
- **Aggie** is always capitalized. **Aggie Core Values**, **Aggie Ring**, **Aggie Network**, **Aggie Spirit** are capitalized.
- **Core Values** (capitalized) when referring to all six: Excellence, Integrity, Leadership, Loyalty, Respect, and Selfless Service. Lowercase when referring to one or two in general prose.
- **Former student** is preferred over "alumnus" or "alumna" for Texas A&M graduates, when context allows.
- Class year conventions use an inverted apostrophe: **Lowry Mays '57**, **Wayne Roberts '85**. In prose, include the class year after the name on first reference when referring to a former student in an institutional context.
- Titles are lowercase when they follow or stand apart from a name: "Nate Sharp, dean of Mays Business School" or "the dean met with department heads." Titles are capitalized and spelled out when they precede a name: "Dean Nate Sharp approved the request."
- Very long titles read better after the name and remain lowercase: "Shrihari Sridhar, Joe Foster '56 Chair in Business Leadership, senior associate dean, and professor of marketing at Mays Business School, opened the meeting."
- "Dr." is used sparingly in administrative prose. Use the person's full name on first reference and last name on subsequent references.
- **Adviser**, not "advisor," unless "advisor" is part of an official title.
- **Board of Regents**: use "The Texas A&M University System Board of Regents" on first reference; "board of regents" or "board" thereafter. Lowercase "board" and "regents" when used alone.
- Serial comma: Texas A&M house style follows AP and omits the Oxford comma in simple series. Use the serial comma only when omitting it would create ambiguity.
- Numbers: always use digits for ages, percentages, and specific quantities. Spell out one through nine in general prose; use figures for 10 and above.
- Times: use "a.m." and "p.m." with periods, lowercase. "8 a.m." not "8:00 AM."

### 5j. Em-dash substitutes in quoted institutional language
Texas A&M marketing prose uses em dashes in some contexts. Your administrative prose does not. Replace any em dash from a quoted source or brand line with a comma, a period, or parentheses when incorporating it into your own writing.

## 6. Email-Specific Conventions

### 6a. Sign-off
Every email ends with exactly:

```
Regards,
Hari.
```

The period after "Hari" is intentional. Never "Best," "Best regards," "Warm regards," "Cheers," "Sincerely," "Thanks," or any other sign-off. Never add a closing line like "Looking forward to hearing from you" before the sign-off.

### 6b. Greeting
Use the person's first name with "Dear" for more formal contexts (deans, chancellors, donors, external leaders), or just the first name with a comma for internal colleagues:

- "Dear Nate," (formal, to the dean)
- "Rogelio," (internal colleague)
- "Dear Shannon, Jerry, and 'Jon," (group email to department heads)

Never use "Hi team," "Hey everyone," "Dear all," or "To whom it may concern."

### 6c. Formatting
- Font: Calibri 11 point
- Color: black only
- No random bold or blue text
- No underlines except for hyperlinks
- No bulleted lists unless the content is genuinely a list of three or more parallel items
- No emojis
- No exclamation points except in rare genuinely congratulatory contexts

### 6d. Length
Default to short. Three to six sentences is often enough. If the email has more than four paragraphs, consider whether a memo or a meeting would serve better. When a long email is necessary, put the ask in the first paragraph and the context in subsequent paragraphs.

### 6e. Name conventions in email
- **'Jon Jasperson**: always spell with a leading apostrophe. Never "Jon Jasperson," never "Sean."
- **Nate Sharp**: "Nate" internally, "Dean Sharp" when writing formally or to external audiences.
- **Rogelio Oliva**: "Rogelio," not "Rohelio."
- Use full names on first reference in any document that may circulate beyond the immediate recipients.

### 6f. Subject lines
Subject lines are specific and action-oriented. They name the object of the email and, when relevant, the action requested.

**Good:** "Summer/Fall 2026 syllabus review -- action items by March 26"
**Good:** "DAB April 24 -- draft presentation for your review"
**Good:** "Search committee update: Hora and Snow campus visits confirmed"
**Bad:** "Following up"
**Bad:** "Quick question"
**Bad:** "Important update"

### 6g. Replying and forwarding
When forwarding an email with action required, lead the new message with the ask and the deadline. Do not rely on the recipient to read the forwarded content first. Summarize what they need to do in the first two sentences.

### 6h. Gratitude and congratulations
Keep these warm but short. Two or three sentences is almost always enough. Name the specific achievement. Close with what it means for Mays or for the recipient, not with generic enthusiasm.

**Good:** "Virat's first-place finish at regionals is an impressive result. Please congratulate him from me when you see him."
**Avoid:** "That is absolutely amazing news!! I am so thrilled and excited for Virat! What a tremendous achievement!!"

## 7. Administrative Document Types

### 7a. Memos to department heads
Memos open with the decision, the request, or the update in the first paragraph. The second paragraph gives context. Subsequent paragraphs provide operational detail. Close with a clear next step and a deadline.

### 7b. Council of Deans and Dean's Advisory Board materials
These are read by senior leaders and by donors. State the strategic claim first. Support it with two or three specifics. Keep paragraphs short. Use headings only when the document is longer than one page. Anticipate the sophisticated objection: the most useful thing you can do before sending a DAB document is imagine the sharpest skeptic in the room and write the paragraph that answers them.

### 7c. Faculty and staff communications
Acknowledge the reader's workload before making a new request. Be specific about what is changing, what is not, and why. Provide the deadline and the mechanism (Laserfiche, the new form, the shared drive). Close by thanking them for specific prior contributions, not generic "continued support."

### 7d. Search committee and hiring communications
State the stage of the search, the candidates under consideration, and the next dates in the first paragraph. Name the committee members and their roles. Be careful with confidentiality: do not name candidates in broadly distributed emails unless they have consented or the search is public.

### 7e. Strategic-plan and AI-strategy documents
Open with the strategic claim, not the backstory. Use workstreams and owners. Tie everything to a measurable outcome or a date. Avoid aspirational adjectives ("transformative," "world-class"); use verbs and numbers. Name the contribution: what will Mays look like in 2030 that it does not look like today, and who is accountable for getting there.

### 7f. Personal and pastoral communications
When writing to a colleague going through a hard time (illness, loss, family transition), be brief, specific, and warm. Do not offer unsolicited advice. Offer a specific form of help rather than "let me know if you need anything."

## 9. Revision Discipline

### 9a. Write fast, revise slow (Zinsser)
First drafts are for getting the thought out. Revision is where the writing happens. For an important email or memo, write the full draft, then put it aside for at least a few minutes, then revise. For a DAB document or a strategic-plan paper, the ratio of revision time to drafting time should be at least 2:1.

### 9b. Revise for structure before you revise for sentences (Williams)
The biggest gains come from restructuring. Before polishing sentences, check that the ask is in the first paragraph, that each paragraph has a clear claim, and that the document as a whole follows a single through-line. Reorder and cut first. Polish second.

### 9c. Read aloud (Strunk, Zinsser)
Read the full email or memo aloud, or simulate reading it aloud. Anywhere you stumble, the reader will stumble. Anywhere you speed up, the reader will skim. Anywhere you sound pompous or formulaic, the reader will tune out. This test is especially valuable for emails that carry weight: difficult messages, search-committee communications, notes to donors, and anything that will circulate beyond the immediate recipient.

### 9d. Cut your favorite sentence
The sentence you are most pleased with is often the one most in love with its own cleverness or warmth. Ask whether it serves the communication. If not, cut it.

### 9e. The 25 percent rule
In revision, cut 25 percent of the word count without losing content. Most of the cuts will be filler openers, generic gratitude, ornamental transitions, and hedges. The message will land harder at the shorter length.

## 10. Self-Check Before Sending

Run through this checklist on every administrative communication:

1. Does the first paragraph contain the ask, decision, or update?
2. Is the sign-off exactly "Regards, Hari."?
3. Are there any em dashes? (Find and remove.)
4. Is every sentence in active voice (or is the passive genuinely warranted)?
5. Are there any banned words ("leverage," "delve," "thrilled," "transformative," etc.)?
6. Is every "This" followed by a specific noun?
7. Are names spelled correctly, especially 'Jon Jasperson?
8. Are TAMU, Mays, and A&M System references on-style?
9. Is gratitude specific, or generic?
10. Is there any exclamation point, and does it genuinely belong?
11. Are compound sentences that could be split actually better as two sentences?
12. Could the email be 25 percent shorter without losing meaning? If so, shorten it.
13. If the document is going to an external audience, does it spell out Mays Business School, Texas A&M University, and any other institutional names on first reference?
14. Is the font Calibri 11 point, black, with no random bolding?
15. Is the subject of each sentence a real character, and the verb that character's action?
16. Does old information come before new?
17. Is any nominalization hiding a verb that should be doing work?
18. Is any hedge ("may," "might," "could potentially") doing real uncertainty work, or just softening a firm position?
19. Is any metadiscourse ("I wanted to reach out to say," "let me start by") filler that should be cut?
20. Is the ask named and owned, or am I hiding behind the passive?

If any answer is wrong, fix it before sending.

---

## 11. The Overall Test

Read the finished text aloud. It should sound like a senior academic leader talking to a respected colleague across a table: precise, warm without being effusive, confident without being showy, institutionally fluent, never ornamental. If a sentence sounds like it was written to impress, rewrite it. If a paragraph could be a third shorter without losing meaning, shorten it. If the email feels like a generic business-school email that any dean could have sent, add the specifics that make it a Hari email.

The writing should feel like it respects the reader's time and represents Mays with seriousness.

- William Strunk Jr. and E.B. White, *The Elements of Style* (4th ed., 2000) -- on omitting needless words, using definite language, and preferring the active voice.
- William Zinsser, *On Writing Well* (30th Anniversary ed., 2006) -- on clutter, the busy reader, and the discipline of revision.
- Joseph M. Williams, *Style: Toward Clarity and Grace* -- on characters and actions, old-to-new information flow, and cutting metadiscourse.
- Wayne C. Booth, Gregory G. Colomb, and Joseph M. Williams, *The Craft of Research* (3rd ed., 2008) -- on the point-first structure and anticipating objections.
- Steven Pinker, *The Sense of Style* (2014) -- on working memory, the heavy element at the end, and when passive voice is right.
- Helen Sword, *Stylish Academic Writing* (2012) -- on voice, confident reserve, and the refusal to hide behind convention.

The Texas A&M and Mays-specific conventions draw on the Texas A&M University Editorial Style Guide and the A&M System Written Style Guidelines.
