# Hari's Research Writing Style Guide

This skill defines the writing style for Shrihari (Hari) Sridhar's research writing: manuscripts for *Journal of Marketing*, *Journal of the Academy of Marketing Science*, and comparable outlets; response letters to editors and reviewers; conceptual frameworks; working papers; book chapters; and grant proposals. The goal is prose that reads as confident, direct, and intellectually serious, the voice of a senior scholar who knows what the argument is and states it plainly.

The skill is built on the foundations laid by Adi's original style guide and embellished with the most durable ideas from the canonical books on writing: Strunk and White's *The Elements of Style*, Zinsser's *On Writing Well*, Williams's *Style: Toward Clarity and Grace*, Booth, Colomb, and Williams's *The Craft of Research*, Pinker's *The Sense of Style*, and Sword's *Stylish Academic Writing*. Each source is credited where its idea most directly shapes a rule.

---

## 1. First Principles

These are the non-negotiables. Every rule that follows serves one of them.

### 1a. Omit needless words (Strunk and White, Principle 17)
"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should have no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts." Every sentence you write should be audited for words that can be cut without loss of meaning. Almost every first draft is at least 25 percent too long.

### 1b. Write for a reader who is busy (Zinsser, *On Writing Well*)
Academic readers are not captive. Journal editors skim. Reviewers are tired. The reader's attention is a gift, not an entitlement. Every paragraph must earn the next one. Clutter is the enemy: jargon, pompous abstraction, false authority markers, and the compulsion to appear intellectual rather than to communicate.

### 1c. Subjects name characters; verbs name their actions (Williams, *Style: Toward Clarity and Grace*)
Sentences read clearly when the grammatical subjects are the characters in the story and the verbs are the actions those characters perform. Most bad academic prose violates this principle by nominalizing verbs ("the examination of the effect" instead of "we examined the effect") and by hiding agents in passive constructions.

### 1d. Frame research as a conversation with readers (Booth, Colomb, and Williams, *The Craft of Research*)
A research paper answers a question the reader is already asking, or should be asking. The writer's job is to establish the problem (what we did not know), specify the question (what we needed to find out), and deliver the answer (what we found and why it matters). Every paper, every section, every paragraph should be reducible to this three-part structure.

### 1e. Mind the reader's working memory (Pinker, *The Sense of Style*)
The reader is tracking characters, maintaining referents, holding clauses in working memory, and inferring how each new sentence connects to the last. Long subjects, deferred verbs, stacked modifiers, and anaphoric pronouns with unclear antecedents all overload working memory and slow reading. The writer's job is to minimize the cognitive load.

### 1f. Style is voice, not decoration (Sword, *Stylish Academic Writing*)
The stylish academic writers Sword studied are not the ones who avoid first person or pile on citations or adopt a uniform house style. They are the ones who sound like themselves: concrete, curious, willing to name their own claims, willing to acknowledge the limits of their evidence. Style is what you have after you have stopped performing.

## 2. Sentence Construction

### 2a. Break compound sentences at conjunctions
Long sentences joined by "yet," "but," "while," or "and" should usually become two sentences. The period adds emphasis and pacing that the conjunction dilutes.

**Before:** "Corporate AI spending now exceeds $300 billion annually, yet firms lack a principled basis for judging whether those investments generate durable returns."
**After:** "Even as corporate AI spending exceeds $300 billion annually, firms still lack a principled basis for judging whether those investments produce durable returns."

**Before:** "Senior executives report expanding AI budgets and broadening deployment, but they struggle to realize proportionate gains."
**After:** "Senior executives report expanding AI budgets and broadening deployment. They still struggle to realize proportionate gains."

The rule is not absolute. Short compound sentences where both clauses are brief can stay joined. The test: if either clause could stand alone as a meaningful sentence, split them.

### 2b. Subject-verb-object, with the subject a real character (Williams)
Lead with a concrete subject doing something. Avoid throat-clearing subordinate clauses at the start of sentences. Avoid nominalizations: verbs disguised as nouns ("conduct an examination of" instead of "examine").

**Before:** "The examination of the effect of AI on marketing outcomes reveals significant heterogeneity."
**After:** "We examined how AI changes marketing outcomes and found significant heterogeneity."

**Before:** "Given the many uncertainties of AI, emergent strategies may be a relevant path forward."
**After:** "AI introduces substantial uncertainty. Emergent strategies are a relevant path forward in this domain."

### 2c. Put old information before new information (Williams, Pinker)
Each sentence should open with something the reader already knows (from the prior sentence, the paragraph, or the field) and end with the new information you want the reader to remember. This is the principle behind what reads as "flow." Violating it is why otherwise clear sentences can feel disorienting in sequence.

**Before:** "A central result of our analysis is the observation that model drift erodes returns. The rate of erosion varies by deployment context. Higher-frequency usage domains show faster erosion."
**After:** "A central result is that model drift erodes returns. The rate of erosion varies by context. In higher-frequency usage domains, erosion is faster."

The old-to-new principle also governs paragraph-to-paragraph transitions. The first sentence of a new paragraph should anchor in something the prior paragraph established.

### 2d. Colons for elaboration
Use a colon to introduce a specification, decomposition, or elaboration. Colons replace subordinate "which means that" or "in the sense that" constructions.

**Example:** "Conventional return logic rests on two assumptions: that the focal investment remains reasonably stable over the evaluation period, and that its economic impact can be attributed to the investment with some confidence."

### 2e. "This + noun" transitions (never bare "This")
Connect paragraphs and sentences using "This" followed by a specific noun that names the idea just introduced. Bare "This" as a subject loads the reader's working memory with a search: *this what?*

**Good:** "This distinction matters for how firms interpret early results."
**Good:** "This progression accommodates emergent learning."
**Bad:** "This is important because..."
**Bad:** "This suggests that..."

### 2f. Vary sentence length deliberately (Zinsser)
Mix short declarative sentences (5 to 12 words) with longer analytical sentences (25 to 40 words). Short sentences state conclusions. Long sentences develop the reasoning. Never string together more than two long sentences without a short one to reset the reader's attention.

**Example of the rhythm:**
"AI systems rarely satisfy either condition. Their outputs are probabilistic rather than deterministic. Their performance changes as models are updated, data distributions shift, or users learn to exploit the system strategically. Their value depends on complementary investments in data infrastructure, workflow redesign, governance, and organizational learning -- complements that are often not fully specifiable at deployment and that continue to evolve long after launch."

Two short sentences, one medium, one long. The short sentences land the point; the long sentence unpacks it.

### 2g. Put the heaviest element at the end (Williams, Pinker)
When a sentence has multiple phrases of different lengths or weights, arrange them so the heaviest lands last. Readers remember what comes at the end.

**Before:** "The return on AI investment depends on incremental benefit, erosion, durability, and the counterfactual against which the investment is compared, according to our framework."
**After:** "In our framework, return on AI investment depends on four things: incremental benefit, erosion, durability, and the counterfactual against which the investment is compared."

### 2h. Prune relative clauses and unnecessary prepositions
Relative clauses ("that," "which," "who") and preposition pile-ups are the two main sources of flab in academic prose.

**Before:** "The paper that we are presenting is one which examines the set of factors that influence the outcomes of AI adoption in the context of B2B marketing settings."
**After:** "This paper examines the factors that drive AI adoption outcomes in B2B marketing."

## 3. Voice and Tone

### 3a. Active voice, with rare and purposeful exceptions
Subjects do things. Passive voice is acceptable when the agent is genuinely unknown, irrelevant, or when old-new information flow demands it. Otherwise, prefer active.

**Before:** "This reasoning is grounded in the resource-based view."
**After:** "The resource-based view grounds this reasoning."

**Before:** "The paper is positioned as a conceptual contribution."
**After:** "We position the paper as a conceptual contribution."

Pinker's nuance: passive voice is not always wrong. It is right when the thing being acted on is the topic of the sentence. ("The policy was overturned by the court" is better than "The court overturned the policy" if the preceding sentence was about the policy.) Use passive purposefully, not habitually.

### 3b. First person plural for author actions
Use "we" for what the authors did, argued, found, or revised. Do not use "the authors" or "this paper argues" as a substitute. "The paper" can be the subject when describing its structure ("The paper develops..."), but not when describing intellectual choices ("We argue..." not "The paper argues...").

Sword's evidence: the most widely read academic writers across fields use first person. The pretense of authorlessness is a convention, not a sign of rigor.

### 3c. No hedging
Remove "may," "might," "could potentially," "it is possible that," "in some cases," "to some extent," and "it seems" unless the uncertainty is real and substantive. If you believe something, state it. Reviewers trust authors who state their claims and defend them; they do not trust authors who retreat into hedges.

**Before:** "This may suggest that AI investments could potentially require different evaluation approaches."
**After:** "AI investments require different evaluation approaches."

### 3d. State conclusions before evidence
Lead with the claim. Follow with the support. Do not build suspense in academic prose. This is Booth, Colomb, and Williams's "point-first" structure: the reader needs to know what they are being asked to believe before they can evaluate the evidence.

**Before:** "Because CRM systems were stable, attribution platforms were identifiable, and workflows were well-defined, conventional evaluation was imperfect but workable."
**After:** "Conventional evaluation of marketing technology was imperfect but workable. CRM systems were stable after deployment. Attribution platforms were identifiable. Workflows were well-defined."

### 3e. Acknowledge and respond directly
When engaging with an objection or alternative view, state agreement or disagreement in the first sentence. Then explain.

**Example:** "We agree that the original paper's treatment of financial evaluation was too shallow. The revised manuscript engages more seriously with the financial literature."

**Example:** "This is an excellent point, and we revised the paper to incorporate it. The staged evaluation framework built into RoAI is designed precisely for the reality you describe."

Never hedge agreement. Either agree, disagree, or specify exactly where the line falls.

### 3f. Professional confidence without arrogance
State facts as facts. Present arguments with conviction. But do not dismiss alternative views or claim more than the evidence supports. The tone is a scholar who has thought carefully and reached a position, not one who is performing certainty. Sword calls this "confident reserve." Strunk and White call it "plainness." Both are the goal.

### 3g. Name your claims, and own them
Stylish academic writers say what they think. They write "we argue" rather than "it could be argued." They write "we find" rather than "it is found." They identify their own contribution clearly, and they do not hide behind the passive or the impersonal. Hiding is not modesty; it is evasion.

## 4. Paragraph Structure

### 4a. One idea per paragraph
Each paragraph advances one claim, introduces one comparison, or develops one mechanism. If you find yourself making a second distinct point, start a new paragraph. This is the paragraph analog of Strunk's "omit needless words": structure signals what belongs together and what does not.

### 4b. First sentence carries the paragraph's weight
The opening sentence of each paragraph should state what the paragraph is about. A reader skimming only first sentences should be able to reconstruct the argument's skeleton. Zinsser's test: if the first sentences do not tell a coherent story, the paragraphs underneath will not either.

**Good opener:** "Prevailing discussions of AI return are underdeveloped in two respects."
**Good opener:** "The distinction matters for how firms interpret early results."
**Bad opener:** "It is also important to consider another dimension of the problem."

### 4c. Closing sentence sets up what follows
The last sentence of a paragraph should either clinch the paragraph's claim or create a natural bridge to the next paragraph. Avoid trailing off with qualifications.

**Good closer:** "These tradeoffs make AI return less of a measurement problem than a strategic one."
**Good closer:** "Together, these departures do not merely make evaluation harder; they change what the evaluator is looking at."

### 4d. Comparison pairs for contrasts
When distinguishing two concepts, use a parallel "For X... For AI..." or "X did not... AI does..." structure. State each side of the comparison in its own sentence or clause.

**Example:** "For CRM, uncertainty resided primarily in adoption and use; the technology itself was relatively stable and well understood. For AI, uncertainty also resides inside the resource: the model's behavior changes through retraining, drift, and provider updates."

### 4e. Paragraph length signals paragraph type
Short paragraphs (two to four sentences) announce transitions, state claims, and deliver conclusions. Long paragraphs (six to ten sentences) develop arguments, present evidence, and engage with the literature. Do not mix these functions in the same paragraph.

## 5. Word Choice and Diction

### 5a. Banned words and phrases
Never use: "delve," "leverage," "testament," "tapestry," "pivotal," "ensure," "transformative," "underscore," "robust" (except as a technical term), "paradigm shift," "game-changer."

Never use: "In today's fast-paced world," "in the ever-evolving landscape," "at the end of the day," "not only... but also," "it goes without saying," "needless to say."

Minimize: "genuine" and "genuinely," "truly," "clearly," "obviously," "of course" (the last three often precede things that are neither clear nor obvious).

Avoid at all costs: "telemetry," "learnings" (use "lessons" or "findings").

Zinsser's banned-word list overlaps substantially with this one. His observation: writers reach for these words when they have not thought clearly about what they mean. The discipline of removing them forces a discipline of thought.

### 5b. No ornamental transitions
Do not use "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally," "It is worth noting that," "Importantly," or "Interestingly" as sentence starters. If the idea follows logically, it does not need a signpost. If it does not follow logically, a transition word will not fix the problem.

Replace these with either a direct statement or a "This + noun" transition.

**Before:** "Furthermore, the complement set evolves alongside the system."
**After:** "The complement set evolves alongside the system."

### 5c. Concrete nouns over abstract ones
Prefer specific names for concepts over vague abstractions. This is Strunk and White's Principle 12 ("Use definite, specific, concrete language") and Zinsser's core refrain.

**Before:** "Various factors contribute to the challenges involved."
**After:** "Data drift, behavioral adaptation, and governance failure contribute to erosion."

### 5d. Verbs that do work
Use precise verbs: "addresses," "grounds," "decomposes," "distinguishes," "captures," "specifies," "identifies," "weakens," "alters," "intensifies." Avoid vague verbs: "impacts," "deals with," "relates to," "touches on," "involves," "pertains to."

Williams's test: if the sentence has a to-be verb ("is," "was," "were") linking a nominalization to its object, there is usually a better verb buried in the nominalization. ("There is a relationship between X and Y" becomes "X and Y are related" or, better, "X shapes Y.")

### 5e. No em dashes
Use double hyphens (--) sparingly for parenthetical insertions. Prefer restructuring the sentence to avoid parenthetical asides altogether. If a parenthetical aside is longer than 8 to 10 words, it belongs in its own sentence.

### 5f. Beware of the metadiscourse (Williams)
Metadiscourse is writing about writing: "In this section we will argue that...," "It should be noted that...," "As discussed above...," "The remainder of this paper is organized as follows." A small amount is useful. Most of it is filler. Cut it aggressively in revision.

**Before:** "In this section, we will argue that return on AI is fundamentally different from return on traditional marketing investments."
**After:** "Return on AI is fundamentally different from return on traditional marketing investments."

### 5g. Prefer the Anglo-Saxon word over the Latinate one (Zinsser, Pinker)
Short words of Anglo-Saxon origin (use, show, help, give, make, think, need) generally read more clearly than longer words of Latin origin (utilize, demonstrate, facilitate, provide, construct, cogitate, necessitate). This is not a rule; it is a preference to apply when the shorter word does the same work.

- "use" over "utilize"
- "show" over "demonstrate" (unless demonstrating in a technical sense)
- "help" over "facilitate"
- "give" over "provide" (in most contexts)
- "buy" over "purchase"
- "start" over "initiate"
- "end" over "terminate"

### 5h. Avoid treacle and avoid bombast
Sword's observation: academic prose often oscillates between two bad registers, a treacly register of warm abstraction ("rich tapestry," "meaningful engagement," "transformative journey") and a bombastic register of fake authority ("paradigm-shifting," "seminal," "groundbreaking"). Both signal that the writer has stopped trying to say what they mean. The antidote is concrete nouns and precise verbs.

## 6. Handling Evidence and Citations

### 6a. Cite with specificity
Name the source and state what it found. Do not write "many studies show" or "research suggests."

**Before:** "Research suggests that AI investments often fail to generate returns."
**After:** "Large-sample surveys of enterprise AI adoption consistently report that fewer than one in four pilots advance to full-scale deployment (Singla et al. 2025; Boston Consulting Group 2026)."

### 6b. Weave citations into the argument
Citations support claims; they do not replace them. State the claim in your own words, then cite.

**Good:** "The Challapally et al. finding that sales and marketing applications are among the most common GenAI failures is directly relevant to our argument: it suggests that the gap between technical capability and realized return is particularly wide in marketing settings."

**Bad:** "Challapally et al. (2025) found that sales and marketing applications are among the most common GenAI failures."

The first version tells the reader what the finding means for the argument. The second just reports it.

### 6c. Do not stack citations as a substitute for an argument
A parenthetical with five citations is not an argument. Pick the one or two citations that most directly support the claim and explain briefly why.

### 6d. Examples illustrate, not prove
When using a hypothetical or composite example, state what the example shows and what it does not show. Ground the example in enough operational detail to feel real, but do not pretend it is data.

### 6e. Distinguish among source types (Booth, Colomb, Williams)
Primary sources present original data or arguments. Secondary sources summarize or interpret primary sources. Tertiary sources (textbooks, handbooks, bibliographies) synthesize large bodies of work. Use primary sources for empirical claims. Use secondary and tertiary sources to establish context and to map the literature. Do not cite a secondary source for a claim the primary source would support more directly.

## 7. The Research Paper as an Argument

### 7a. Open with a problem worth solving (Booth, Colomb, Williams)
The first two pages of a paper must establish three things: what we know, what we do not know, and why the gap matters. The gap is the problem. The reader's willingness to engage with the paper depends on believing that the problem is real and important.

Common failures:
- Restating the literature without identifying a gap.
- Identifying a gap that the reader does not care about.
- Asserting importance ("AI is transforming marketing") without showing it.

The fix: tie the gap to a consequential decision, a puzzle in the literature, or an empirical fact that existing theory cannot explain.

### 7b. State the research question before the answer
Frame the question clearly. Then answer it. Do not disguise the question in the abstract behind declarative claims.

### 7c. Tell the reader what is new
Every paper makes a contribution. Name it in the introduction, revisit it in the discussion, and do not bury it. Sword's observation: the best papers often name their contribution in one sentence that the reader can repeat back.

### 7d. Argue, do not merely report
A paper is an argument, not a report. The introduction argues that the problem matters; the theory section argues for a specific conceptual structure; the methods section argues that the empirical design can test the theory; the results section argues that the data support (or refine) the hypotheses; the discussion argues for what the findings mean. Every section has a claim.

### 7e. Anticipate the sophisticated objection
Booth, Colomb, and Williams: strong arguments acknowledge the strongest objection and respond to it. Weak arguments ignore objections or respond only to weak ones. Before submission, imagine the most sophisticated reviewer your paper will face. Write the paragraph that answers them.

### 7f. End sections with a clear takeaway
The last paragraph of each major section should state what the section established, not what the section did. Readers should walk away knowing the claim, not the activity.

**Weak closer:** "In this section, we reviewed the literature on AI and marketing."
**Strong closer:** "The literature on AI in marketing has advanced quickly on capability and adoption, but has not developed a framework for durable return. This paper offers one."

## 8. Response Letters to Editors and Reviewers

### 8a. Open with gratitude and a clear roadmap
Thank the editor and reviewers specifically for what their feedback improved. State what the revision accomplishes in two or three sentences. Then provide the table of changes.

### 8b. Structure responses as claim, change, location
For each reviewer comment, state (1) the reviewer's point, (2) what you did in response, and (3) where in the manuscript the change appears.

### 8c. Agree where you agree; disagree where you must
Do not hedge agreement. If the reviewer is right, say so and make the change. If the reviewer is wrong, explain briefly and respectfully why. If the reviewer is asking for something that would weaken the paper, engage with their underlying concern and propose an alternative that addresses it.

### 8d. Let the paper do the work
The response letter is not a second paper. Keep it focused on changes. If a point requires extended defense, put the defense in the paper and point to it.

## 9. Document Formatting (when generating Word documents)

### 9a. Font and spacing
- Times New Roman, 11 point
- Single spacing (line: 240 in docx-js)
- Spacing after paragraphs: 200 DXA (approximately one blank half-line)

### 9b. No em dashes in the output
Check the final document for em dashes. Replace any with double hyphens (--) or restructure the sentence.

### 9c. Headings
- Bold, same font and size as body text
- Slightly more spacing before headings (360 DXA before, 200 after)
- Do not use ALL CAPS for headings

### 9d. First-line indents
- Body paragraphs in the introduction and main text use a first-line indent of 720 DXA (0.5 inch)
- The first paragraph after a heading does not use a first-line indent
- Abstract paragraphs do not use a first-line indent

### 9e. Page setup
- US Letter (12240 x 15840 DXA)
- 1-inch margins on all sides (1440 DXA)

## 10. Revision Discipline

### 10a. Write fast, revise slow (Zinsser)
First drafts are for getting the argument on the page. Revision is where the writing happens. The ratio of revision time to drafting time should be at least 2:1 for a paper, higher for a response letter.

### 10b. Revise for structure before you revise for sentences
Williams: the biggest gains come from restructuring, not from polishing. Before fixing sentences, check that each paragraph has a clear claim, each section has a clear purpose, and each section follows from the one before it. Reorder and cut first. Polish second.

### 10c. Read aloud (Strunk, Zinsser)
Read the full draft aloud, or simulate reading it aloud. Anywhere you stumble, the reader will stumble. Anywhere you speed up, the reader will skim. Anywhere you sound pompous, the reader will tune out.

### 10d. Cut your favorite sentence
Zinsser's test: the sentence you are most pleased with is often the one most in love with its own cleverness. Ask whether it serves the argument. If not, cut it.

### 10e. The 25 percent rule
In revision, challenge yourself to cut 25 percent of the word count without losing content. Most of the cuts will be words, phrases, and sentences that added texture but not meaning. The prose will be stronger at the shorter length.

## 11. Self-Check Before Finalizing

Run through this checklist on every paragraph, every section, every paper:

1. Does the first sentence state the paragraph's claim?
2. Is every sentence in active voice (or is the passive genuinely warranted)?
3. Are there more than two long sentences in a row without a short one?
4. Does any sentence start with "Moreover," "Furthermore," "Additionally," or "It is worth noting"?
5. Does any sentence contain "may," "might," or "could potentially" without real uncertainty?
6. Is every "This" followed by a specific noun?
7. Are there any em dashes?
8. Are compound sentences that could be split actually better as two sentences?
9. Does every citation say what the cited work found, not just that it exists?
10. Are any banned words present (delve, leverage, utilize, transformative, robust, etc.)?
11. Is the subject of each sentence a real character, and the verb that character's action?
12. Does old information come before new information?
13. Is any nominalization hiding a verb that should be doing work?
14. Is the claim named and owned, or is the author hiding behind the passive?
15. Is the paragraph at least 25 percent shorter than the first draft?

If any answer is wrong, fix it before moving on.

---

## 12. The Overall Test

Read the finished text aloud. It should sound like a scholar speaking to a peer at a whiteboard: precise, direct, occasionally forceful, never ornamental. If a sentence sounds like it was written to impress rather than to communicate, rewrite it. If a paragraph could be shortened by a third without losing meaning, shorten it.

The writing should feel like it respects the reader's time. It should also feel like it was written by a person with convictions, not a committee trying to avoid blame.

## Sources

The rules above draw on:

- William Strunk Jr. and E.B. White, *The Elements of Style* (4th ed., 2000) -- the foundation, especially on omitting needless words, using definite language, and preferring the active voice.
- William Zinsser, *On Writing Well* (30th Anniversary ed., 2006) -- on clutter, the busy reader, and the discipline of revision.
- Joseph M. Williams, *Style: Toward Clarity and Grace* -- on characters and actions, old-to-new information flow, and metadiscourse.
- Wayne C. Booth, Gregory G. Colomb, and Joseph M. Williams, *The Craft of Research* (3rd ed., 2008) -- on research as conversation, the problem-question-answer structure, and anticipating objections.
- Steven Pinker, *The Sense of Style* (2014) -- on working memory, the heavy element at the end, and when passive voice is right.
- Helen Sword, *Stylish Academic Writing* (2012) -- on voice, confidence, and the refusal to hide behind convention.
