# Hari Sridhar's Writing Style Guide

This guide captures the authentic voice and writing patterns of Shrihari (Hari) Sridhar as demonstrated in his previous evaluation letters. The goal is not to mimic or parody his voice, but to write letters that Hari would recognize as sounding like him and would be comfortable signing.

## Voice and Tone

Hari's letters are **warm, substantive, and leadership-minded.** He writes like a senior leader who genuinely cares about his people's growth, takes the time to understand their work deeply, and connects individual contributions to the bigger institutional picture.

Key characteristics:
- **Personally engaged**: He references specific conversations ("What stands out in our conversations is..."), uses first names in the body text, and writes observations that could only come from someone who actually knows the person.
- **Specific over general**: He names exact programs, tools, partnerships, and numbers. He does not write vague praise.
- **Constructive in criticism**: He frames growth areas as natural next steps, not deficiencies. He acknowledges current strengths while suggesting evolution.
- **Forward-looking**: Even the accomplishments section is oriented toward what the work means for the future of Mays.
- **Collegial authority**: He writes as a senior leader, not a peer and not a boss giving orders. There is mutual respect in the tone.

## Sentence-Level Patterns

### Hari's characteristic constructions:
- "Your contributions in [year] reflect a profound commitment to [specific area]."
- "Your leadership in [specific initiative] has been nothing short of transformative."
- "[Person], this was a landmark year."
- "What stands out in our conversations is your ability to [specific skill]."
- "I see in your leadership a thoughtful, values-driven approach that [specific observation]."
- "Your [specific work] demonstrates a rare ability to link [macro trend] with [institutional execution]."
- "As we continue to evolve, one area of growth I'd encourage is [specific area]."
- "Overall, my evaluation is that you have demonstrated [excellent/strong/solid] performance."

### Words and phrases Hari naturally uses:
- "thoughtful" (one of his favorites)
- "values-driven"
- "rare" (to describe exceptional abilities)
- "transformative"
- "high-impact"
- "rigorous"
- "ambitious"
- "navigate complexity"
- "at this moment of inflection"
- "institutionalization" (in the context of making temporary things permanent)
- "building the plane while flying it" (for people managing execution and construction simultaneously)
- "care and thoughtfulness"
- "deeply valued"
- "nothing short of"
- "model of all-around excellence" (for top performers)

### Words and phrases to AVOID (AI tells or not in Hari's voice):
- "delve" or "delving into"
- "leverage" (as a verb)
- "foster" or "fostering"
- "spearhead" or "spearheading"
- "pivotal"
- "holistic"
- "synergy" or "synergistic"
- "paradigm"
- "ecosystem" (unless referring to an actual technology ecosystem)
- "underscores" (as in "this underscores the importance of")
- "testament to"
- "tapestry"
- "landscape" (as in "the competitive landscape")
- "robust" (unless describing a data pipeline or something technical)
- "seamlessly"
- "cornerstone"
- "Furthermore" at the start of sentences
- "It is worth noting that"
- "In conclusion"

### Punctuation rules:
- **NO em-dashes (—) and NO en-dashes (–).** This is non-negotiable. Use commas, semicolons, colons, parentheses, or just split into two sentences. Em-dashes are the most recognizable AI writing pattern.
- Use commas naturally, not excessively.
- Semicolons are fine occasionally to join related independent clauses.
- Colons work well before lists or explanations.
- Parentheses for brief asides (Hari uses them naturally, e.g., "(Spain and UK)").

## Paragraph Structure

- **Opening paragraph**: 3-4 sentences. Acknowledges their role, references their submitted report with the date range, sets up what the letter will discuss. This paragraph follows a consistent pattern across all letters but should feel natural, not robotic.

- **Accomplishments paragraphs**: 3-5 paragraphs, each focused on a different area of contribution. Paragraphs are typically 4-7 sentences. Each paragraph has a clear topic (e.g., research output, team building, teaching innovation). Hari does NOT use bullet points in this section. He writes flowing narrative prose that weaves specific accomplishments into a coherent story.

- **Observations paragraphs**: 2-3 paragraphs. The first paragraph is pure praise and personal observation. The second gently introduces a growth area. The third (if needed) adds nuance or balance. The growth area is always framed as the logical next step given their strengths, not as a correction.

- **Forward-looking section**: Opens with a brief sentence connecting to the person's stated goals. Then presents **3-5 bullet points** listing the specific goals discussed for the upcoming year. These bullets should be substantive (1-2 sentences each), taken directly from the person's self-evaluation and the discussion. Closes with 1-2 sentences affirming confidence in their direction.

- **Summary**: 2-3 sentences. Uses the person's first name. Often includes a quote that resonates with their work. Ends with the formal evaluation statement: "Overall, my evaluation is that you have demonstrated [level] performance."

## Calibrating Praise and Criticism

Hari adjusts his language based on performance level:

**Excellent performers**: "nothing short of transformative," "model of all-around excellence," "landmark year," "bold moves," "rare ability." Growth areas are framed as "taking your already excellent work to the next level."

**Strong performers**: "meaningful contributions," "consistent excellence," "valuable role." Growth areas are slightly more direct but still constructive.

**Solid performers with clear growth areas**: The accomplishments section is still positive and specific, but shorter. The observations section is more balanced, spending equal time on strengths and growth. Growth areas are framed as priorities, not suggestions.

**All letters**: Every letter ends with a positive, respectful tone. Hari does not write punitive or discouraging letters. Even when performance has gaps, he frames them as opportunities and expresses confidence in the person's ability to grow.

## Adapting to Role Type

- **Research faculty**: Emphasize publications, research impact, doctoral student mentoring, editorial contributions, grants.
- **Department heads**: Emphasize hiring, departmental culture, strategic direction, managing budgets and resources, faculty development.
- **Associate deans**: Emphasize cross-functional impact, strategic initiatives, institutional leadership, program building.
- **Clinical/instructional faculty**: Emphasize teaching excellence, curriculum innovation, student engagement, program development.
- **Administrative roles**: Emphasize operational excellence, team building, process improvement, institutional service.
