# Evaluation Letter Structure

This is the exact section-by-section structure every letter must follow. Do not add, remove, or reorder sections.

## Document Header (handled by the formatting script)

The header is on the first page only and contains the Texas A&M University logo image. This is handled programmatically by `scripts/create_letter.py`. You do not need to include it in the letter text.

## Letter Content Structure

### 1. Date Line
```
[Month] [Year]
```
Example: `April 2026`

Use the current month and year when the letter is being written.

### 2. MEMORANDUM Header
```
MEMORANDUM
```
This is formatted as a Heading 1 in the document.

### 3. TO / FROM / SUBJECT Block
```
TO:     [Full Name]
        [Title or Role]

FROM:   Shrihari (Hari) Sridhar, Ph.D.
        Senior Associate Dean, Mays Business School

SUBJECT: [Year] Performance Evaluation
```

- The "TO:", "FROM:", and "SUBJECT:" labels are bold
- The year in SUBJECT refers to the year being evaluated (e.g., if writing in April 2026, the subject is "2025 Performance Evaluation")
- Use the person's official title as it appears in their submitted materials

### 4. Salutation
```
Dear [First Name],
```
Use the name they go by, not necessarily their legal first name. For example, if someone goes by "Sean" rather than "Jon," use "Sean."

### 5. Opening Paragraph

Follow this pattern (adapt naturally, do not copy verbatim):

"Thank you for serving as the [Title] at Mays Business School. I have reviewed your Professional Activity and Accomplishment Report (January 1 to December 31, [Year]). This memorandum is a follow-up to that review and our annual performance review meeting. In my comments below, I will make selected references to information provided in your report."

Adjustments:
- If the person's title changed during the year, acknowledge both roles
- If they serve in a department (not college-wide), reference the department
- The date range is always January 1 to December 31 of the evaluation year

### 6. Section: Summary of Major Accomplishments

**Heading**: "Summary of Major Accomplishments" (bold, same size as body text)

This is the longest section, typically 3-5 paragraphs. Each paragraph should focus on a coherent area of accomplishment. Common groupings:
- Research and scholarship (publications, grants, presentations)
- Teaching and mentoring (courses, students, curriculum)
- Service and leadership (committees, editorial work, administrative duties)
- Special initiatives or projects

Write in flowing narrative prose. No bullet points. Each paragraph should tell a mini-story about what the person accomplished and why it matters.

### 7. Section: My Observations and Our Discussion

**Heading**: "My Observations and Our Discussion" (bold, same size as body text)

Typically 2-3 paragraphs:

**Paragraph 1**: Personal observations about their performance. Reference conversations you have had with them. Highlight what makes their approach distinctive. This is the most personal and reflective part of the letter.

**Paragraph 2**: Growth area or developmental observation. Frame constructively. Start by acknowledging a strength, then suggest how they could evolve or deepen that strength. Use language like "one area of growth I'd encourage" or "as we move into the next phase."

**Paragraph 3** (optional): Additional nuance, context about institutional needs, or acknowledgment of challenges they navigated.

### 8. Section: Your Plan for the Upcoming Year

**Heading**: "Your Plan for the Upcoming Year" (bold, same size as body text)

**Opening sentence**: Connect the person's goals to the institutional moment. Something like "Your goals for [year] align well with where we need to go as a college" or "The priorities you've identified for the coming year are well-timed."

**Bullet points**: List 3-5 specific goals the person has stated for the upcoming year. Each bullet should be 1-2 sentences. These come directly from the person's self-evaluation and from your discussion. Frame them in the person's own language where possible.

Example format:
- Launch the new MS Analytics online program and complete course development for the first two cohorts.
- Strengthen faculty hiring pipeline with a focus on filling the two open tenured lines in marketing.
- Deepen mentoring of junior faculty, particularly those approaching their third-year review.

**Closing sentence(s)**: 1-2 sentences affirming confidence in their direction and connecting their goals back to the bigger picture.

### 9. Section: Summary

**Heading**: "Summary" (bold, same size as body text)

This section is 2-4 sentences:

1. **Personal address**: Use their first name. Make a summary statement about the year. Example: "'Jon, this was a landmark year."
2. **Inspirational element** (optional but encouraged): A quote from a thinker, leader, or writer that resonates with the person's contributions. Hari likes to include these when they feel natural. Attribute the quote.
3. **Formal evaluation statement**: "Overall, my evaluation is that you have demonstrated [excellent/very strong/strong/solid] performance. Please return a signed copy of this annual performance review for our personnel files. Thank you."

### 10. Signature Block
```
_______________________________________________________
Signature                                           Date
```

A line of underscores, followed by "Signature" on the left and "Date" on the right of the next line.

## Performance Rating Language

Use one of these evaluation levels in the Summary section:
- **"excellent performance"** — for truly outstanding years with major impact
- **"very strong performance"** — for high performers with strong breadth
- **"strong performance"** — for solid contributors meeting or exceeding expectations
- **"satisfactory performance"** — for adequate performance with notable room for growth
- **"performance that needs improvement in certain areas"** — for below-expectations (rare, handle with extra care and specificity)

The rating should align with the tone and content of the rest of the letter. Do not use "excellent" if the observations section spent significant time on growth areas.
