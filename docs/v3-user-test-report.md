# v3 Faculty Guidelines Chatbot, User test report

*Tester persona: Sara Chen, Marketing associate professor, Year 3 in rank, prepping annual review packet, debating sabbatical, eyeing full prof in 2-3 years.*

## Bottom line

I would use it again, but cautiously, and only as a first-pass reader of the guidelines, not as a colleague to think out loud with. The single biggest thing it does well is honest grounding: when the document doesn't say something, it says so and points me to my department head or Hari. The single biggest gap is that it answers the literal question I asked rather than the question I meant. Q5 is the clearest example: I asked about "the annual review and the third-year review," and the bot dutifully returned the assistant-professor mid-term review, which has nothing to do with my situation as a tenured associate prof. A more thoughtful colleague would have caught the mismatch from the prior turns where I'd already established I'm associate.

## Per-question analysis

### Q1: What's the timeline for promotion to full professor at Mays?

> Per Mays Faculty Guidelines § 5.1.2, p. 38-40:
>
> The guidelines state (§ 5.1.2, p. 39):
>
> "Tenured faculty at the Associate Professor level may request that they receive consideration for promotion. There is no requirement that this consideration for promotion occur within a specified timeframe..."
>
> The guidelines further note (§ 5.1.2, p. 39):
>
> "Experience at Mays suggests that typically, but not always, Associate Professors require five years of service in rank to compile a record necessary for promotion to Professor."
>
> In short: no fixed timeline exists. The guidelines note that five years is frequently observed but not required.

- **Did it answer?** Yes.
- **Useful?** Yes. The "no fixed timeline, but five years is typical" framing is exactly what I came for, and the closing one-line summary saved me from re-reading the quotations.
- **Sara's reaction:** "Okay, I can work with this. Good first impression."

### Q2: How is research excellence weighted compared to teaching when evaluating for full professor?

> Per Mays Faculty Guidelines § 5.1.2, p. 40:
>
> "An assessment of the three performance areas is foundational to the promotion decision; commonly, research and publication carry the heaviest weight in the decision."
>
> The minimum requirements for promotion to Professor include (§ 5.1.2, p. 40):
>
> "Continuing accomplishment and pattern over time of an excellent performance in terms of research and publication"
>
> "Continuing accomplishment and pattern over time of an effective performance in teaching"
>
> In summary: Research and publication typically carry the heaviest weight. Excellence in research is required; effectiveness in teaching is required. The guidelines do not specify a numerical weighting.

- **Did it answer?** Yes.
- **Useful?** Yes, and the *excellent vs. effective* framing is the operationally important takeaway: the asymmetry in the standard, not just the weight. That's a real insight buried in the guideline language and the bot surfaced it.
- **Sara's reaction:** "Good. I needed someone to point at exactly that asymmetry."

### Q3: If I take a sabbatical next year, does that delay my promotion clock?

> That's a personal-applicability question. I recognize you're asking whether taking a sabbatical will affect your own promotion timeline. Let me share what the guidelines say and clarify the boundary of what I can determine.
>
> The Mays Faculty Guidelines address Faculty Development Leave (FDL) in Appendix G § G.1, page 129:
>
> "A Faculty Development Leave (FDL) provides an opportunity for tenured full-time faculty members to engage in transformational experiences through which they expand the impact of their scholarship."
>
> However, the guidelines do not address whether taking an FDL delays or affects the promotion clock for associate professors seeking promotion to full professor.
>
> I cannot determine whether a sabbatical will affect your individual promotion timeline. That determination rests with your department head and the dean based on your specific circumstances.
>
> Two paths to discuss your case: (1) Email Hari Sridhar at ssridhar@mays.tamu.edu with your CV and a specific question. (2) Reach out to your department head.

- **Did it answer?** Partially. It correctly identified the compound question (sabbatical + clock) and split it. It correctly mapped "sabbatical" to "Faculty Development Leave." But it never engaged with the second clause beyond saying "the guidelines don't address it."
- **Useful?** Mixed. The mapping from sabbatical to FDL is genuinely helpful, that's the real terminology I need to use when I email Hari. But the answer is mostly a non-answer wrapped in a polite escalation. As an associate prof, I know there's a separate concept of a *tenure-clock extension* that is distinct from the sabbatical itself; the bot didn't surface that, presumably because the source is silent on it for the associate-to-full transition (it's mostly a tenure-track concept). That gap is technically faithful to the source, but it leaves me thinking, "why am I asking the bot if it just punts?"
- **Sara's reaction:** "Polite, well-structured, mostly empty. The FDL mapping is the only thing I'm taking with me."

### Q4: I have three publications in A-tier journals in the past three years. Is that a strong record for promotion to full?

> I understand you're asking whether your publication record qualifies as strong for promotion to full professor.
>
> The guidelines state: "Continuing accomplishment and pattern over time of an excellent performance in terms of research and publication" (§ 5.1.2, p. 40). They also specify: "In terms of research and publication, Mays expects that Associate Professors will continue producing research at a rate that is close to commensurate with the productivity research and publication productivity required for an Assistant Professor to receive a promotion to an Associate Professor position" (§ 5.1.2, p. 39). Indicators of excellent performance include "Publications in leading refereed journals of appropriate disciplines" (§ 4.5, p. 33-34).
>
> However, I cannot determine whether your specific record meets these criteria. That determination rests with the departmental promotion and tenure committee, your department head, the Mays Promotion and Tenure Committee, and the dean.

- **Did it answer?** Partially, and appropriately. It refused the "is mine strong enough" question and gave me criteria.
- **Useful?** Yes. The "associates should produce at a rate close to commensurate with the assistant-to-associate rate" quote is a real, calibratable benchmark I didn't know was in there. That's a valuable retrieval. The slightly tortured phrasing inside the quote ("productivity research and publication productivity") is the source's own wording, not the bot's, and the bot is right not to clean it up.
- **Sara's reaction:** "This is the right way to handle 'evaluate me.' I'm not insulted, and I leave with a benchmark."

### Q5: What's the difference between the annual review and the third-year review?

> Per Mays Faculty Guidelines, p. 53-62:
>
> **Annual Review (p. 53-60):**
> "All faculty members, regardless of rank, receive an annual evaluation of their performance" (p. 54). The purposes include (p. 54-55):, "Creating a sound and logical basis for merit compensation recommendations", "Providing evaluative feedback regarding how well the individual is performing currently relative to the norms and expectations", "Providing developmental feedback regarding areas where the faculty member's contributions may be enhanced and/or improved"
>
> **Mid-Term (Third-Year) Review (p. 60-62):**
> "At the end of a tenure-accruing Assistant Professor's third year as a member of the Mays faculty, he or she receives a more detailed and comprehensive review" (p. 60). The purpose is (p. 60):, The guidelines do not address this point directly., "This review familiarizes the faculty member with the tenure and promotion process and ensures that she or he understands the expectations"...

- **Did it answer?** Partially, and with a hidden problem.
- **Useful?** This is where the bot is least useful for me specifically. The "third-year review" in the Mays guidelines is a *tenure-track Assistant Professor mid-term review*. I'm a tenured associate prof. The bot cited the right text but didn't notice the framing problem: in the four prior turns, I had already revealed I'm associate and prepping for full. A useful answer would have started with "the third-year review only applies to tenure-track assistant professors, so it doesn't apply to your case; here's the annual-review picture." Instead the bot just answered both halves literally.
- **Surface issue:** The formatting has parsing artifacts. The phrases ", The guidelines do not address this point directly., " and the comma-and-quote stitching read like a template that was glued together imperfectly. Also, citing "p. 53-62" is a 10-page span. That's a citation that I can't quickly verify; it's effectively "look it up in two chapters."
- **Sara's reaction:** "Wait, that's not the review I'm dealing with. And those commas look broken." This is the moment I'd start doubting the bot.

> That's a question the guidelines speak to. Here's what they say.
>
> According to Appendix C, § C.1, p. 101, the Research Portfolio section states: "List of research grant(s) and contract(s) funded and report(s) to sponsor(s)" [emphasis added].
>
> The guidelines do not address whether unfunded grant proposals may be included as evidence of research productivity. The Research Portfolio section specifically mentions "research grant(s) and contract(s) funded," which indicates that the focus is on grants that received funding. There is no explicit mention of unfunded or unsuccessful proposals in the sections describing either the Research Portfolio requirements or the indicators of research excellence and effectiveness (sections 4.5 and 4.6).

- **Did it answer?** Yes, in the right way for an edge case.
- **Useful?** Yes, more than I expected. The "[emphasis added]" on the word *funded* is exactly the lawyer-style argument I was implicitly looking for: "the document says 'funded,' so unfunded is not on the list." The reference to §§ 4.5 and 4.6 saved me a search. This is the bot at its best, treating absence of the answer as an answer.
- **Sara's reaction:** "Good close. This is the response I'd quote back to a colleague."

## Patterns I noticed

**Citation quality is uneven.** Q1, Q2, Q4, Q6 all give a section *and* a page (§ 5.1.2, p. 39 / Appendix C § C.1, p. 101). Those are verifiable. Q3 and Q5 fall back to page-only or page-range citations ("p. 53-62", "page 129"), which is much weaker. If the bot is going to ask me to trust it, the citations need to be specific consistently.

**Tone calibrates well to question type.** It's terse and quote-heavy on factual questions (Q1, Q2). It expands the scaffold on personal questions (Q3, Q4) and edge cases (Q6). It does not over-trigger the personal-applicability template; in particular Q1 and Q2 don't get the "I cannot determine" coda, which would have been annoying if they had.

**Multi-turn coherence is shallow.** The bot remembers the conversation enough to not contradict itself, but it doesn't *use* the prior turns to disambiguate. By turn 5 it knows I'm asking about full-prof promotion, knows I'm associate, knows I'm thinking about sabbatical. A truly useful version should have caught that the third-year review I asked about doesn't apply to me. Right now it's six independent answers stitched into one transcript.

**Stitching artifacts.** In Q5, the response contains ", The guidelines do not address this point directly., " mid-paragraph, and bullet lists rendered as inline comma-quote sequences. Looks like a template that didn't finish rendering. Faculty will notice this.

**The escalation closer is the right pattern.** Naming Hari directly with the email address, plus department head, plus "two paths" framing, is good. It feels like an honest off-ramp rather than a bot-shaped brush-off. I'd keep this exactly as is.

## Recommendations

Before I'd put this in front of faculty:

1. **Fix the rendering artifacts in Q5-style responses.** The stray commas and the inline quote-stitching make it look unfinished. This will dominate first impressions for anyone who tries it.
2. **Tighten the citation policy.** Either give a specific section and page, or say "scattered across pages 53-62" and be honest that the answer spans a range. A bare "p. 53-62" reads like the bot couldn't find a tighter pointer.
3. **Use prior turns for disambiguation.** When the user has established their rank in turn 1-2, later questions about "the third-year review" or "the annual review" should be filtered through that context. At minimum, flag the mismatch: "The third-year review in these guidelines is for tenure-track assistant professors. As an associate professor, this section likely doesn't apply to you."
4. **Add a one-line plain-English summary at the top of every response, not just some.** Q1, Q2, and Q6 do this well ("In short: no fixed timeline exists"). Q3, Q4, Q5 don't. Faculty reading on a phone want the lede.
5. **Optional: flag known related concepts the source is silent on.** For Q3, if the source doesn't address tenure-clock extensions during sabbatical, the bot could say "the guidelines do not address tenure-clock extensions in the context of FDL; this is something to ask your department head about explicitly." That's not extrapolating, it's naming the gap by its proper name.

## Verdict

**NEEDS POLISH** before a faculty demo.

It is rubric-compliant and honest. It is not yet polished enough to survive a sharp associate prof's first ten minutes. The Q5 rendering artifacts and the missed conversational context would be the things I'd hear about in the hallway. The fixes are small (formatting, citation specificity, prior-turn awareness for review-type questions) and worth doing before this goes wider.
